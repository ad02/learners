# Pages and Posts

WordPress has two main types of content: **Pages** and **Posts**. They might seem similar, but they serve very different purposes.

## Pages: Your Permanent Content

**Pages** are for content that doesn't change much. Think of them as the rooms in your house — they stay where they are.

Examples of pages:

- **About** — who you are and what you do
- **Contact** — how people can reach you
- **Services** — what you offer
- **Privacy Policy** — legal stuff

Pages don't have dates or categories. They just sit there, quietly doing their job.

## Posts: Your Fresh Content

**Posts** are for content that's timely — things you publish regularly, like a blog. Think of them as letters you send out. Each one has a date, and they appear in order from newest to oldest.

Examples of posts:

- Blog articles
- Company news and announcements
- Tips and tutorials
- Product updates

Posts can be organized with **categories** (broad topics like "Marketing" or "News") and **tags** (specific keywords like "email" or "social media").

### Categories vs Tags

Think of it like organizing a bookstore:
- **Categories** are the sections: Fiction, Non-Fiction, Science, History
- **Tags** are the sticky notes: "Best Seller," "Staff Pick," "On Sale"

Every post should have **one category** (required). Tags are optional but helpful for visitors who want to find related content.

### Featured Images and Excerpts

Two features that make posts look more professional:

- **Featured Image** — the main image that represents your post. It shows up in blog listings, social media shares, and search results. Always set one!
- **Excerpt** — a short summary of your post (1-2 sentences). If you don't write one, WordPress auto-generates it from the first few lines. Writing your own excerpt gives you more control over how the post appears in listings.

## Creating a Page or Post

The process is almost the same for both:

1. Go to **Pages** or **Posts** in the sidebar
2. Click **"Add New"**
3. Type your **title** at the top
4. Write your **content** below
5. Click **"Publish"** when you're ready

It's really that simple.

## The Block Editor (Gutenberg)

WordPress uses something called the **Block Editor** (also known as Gutenberg). Instead of typing everything into one big text box, you build your content with **blocks** — like snapping together LEGO pieces.

### Common Blocks You'll Use

Here are the blocks you'll reach for most often:

- **Paragraph** — regular text (this is the default when you start typing)
- **Heading** — section titles (H2, H3, etc.). Use H2 for main sections, H3 for subsections.
- **Image** — add a photo or graphic. You can drag and drop images directly!
- **List** — bullet points or numbered lists. Great for steps or features.
- **Quote** — a styled blockquote for testimonials or callouts
- **Button** — a clickable call-to-action like "Contact Us" or "Learn More"
- **Columns** — split content side by side (great for comparing things)
- **Spacer** — adds vertical spacing between sections
- **Gallery** — display multiple images in a grid
- **Video** — embed a YouTube or Vimeo video by pasting the URL

### How to Use Blocks

To add a new block:
1. Click the **"+"** button (it appears between blocks or at the top-left)
2. Search for the block type you want
3. Click it to add it to your page

To move blocks around, hover over a block and use the **up/down arrows** on the left, or drag it with the **grip handle** (the six dots).

To delete a block, click on it, then click the **three-dot menu** and choose "Remove block."

## The Media Library

When you add an image block, you can either:

- **Upload** a new image from your computer
- **Choose from the Media Library** — a collection of all images and files you've already uploaded

The Media Library is like a filing cabinet for all your images, PDFs, and documents. Everything you upload lives there and can be reused across multiple pages and posts.

A quick tip: before uploading images, make sure they're not too large. Images over 2MB can slow your site down. A good size is usually under 500KB.

## Try It Yourself

Create a full page using 5 or more blocks:

1. Go to **Pages > Add New**
2. Title it "My Practice Page"
3. Add a **Heading** block (H2) — "Welcome to My Site"
4. Add a **Paragraph** block with a few sentences introducing yourself
5. Add an **Image** block — upload any image or use a placeholder
6. Add a **List** block with 3-4 bullet points about your skills or interests
7. Add a **Button** block that says "Contact Me"
8. Click **Publish** and view the page!

Now create a Post:
1. Go to **Posts > Add New**
2. Title it "My First Blog Post"
3. Write a paragraph about something you learned today
4. Set a **category** (create one called "Learning")
5. Add a **featured image**
6. Click **Publish**

## Tips and Common Mistakes

- **Use Pages for permanent content, Posts for regular updates** — don't create blog articles as pages. It makes your site harder to manage.
- **Always set a featured image on posts** — posts without images look incomplete in listings and social shares.
- **Don't skip headings** — go H2, H3, H4 in order. Don't jump from H2 to H5. Search engines and screen readers use heading structure.
- **Preview before publishing** — click "Preview" to see how your content looks on the live site before hitting Publish.
- **Draft mode is your friend** — if you're not ready to publish, save as a draft. You can come back anytime.
- **Name your images** — rename image files before uploading (use `team-photo.jpg` instead of `IMG_4523.jpg`). Better for SEO and organization.

## Why This Matters

Pages and posts are the core of every WordPress site. Whether you're building a business website, a blog, or a portfolio, you'll use these two content types constantly. Mastering the block editor now will save you hours of frustration later.

## Practice Scenario

Imagine you're building a website for a local coffee shop. Plan out:
1. What **pages** would you create? (Hint: About, Menu, Contact, Location...)
2. What **posts** would you write? (Hint: New menu items, events, holiday hours...)
3. What **categories** would organize the posts?

Write down your answers. This planning exercise is exactly what you'd do before building a real site.
