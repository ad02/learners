# Domains, Hosting & DNS

You know that websites are just files. But where do those files live, and how does your browser find them? Let's break it down with simple analogies.

## Domains: Your Website's Address

A **domain** is your website's address — like a street address for a house.

- `google.com` is Google's address
- `youtube.com` is YouTube's address
- `learners.academy` could be ours!

Just like you can't have two houses with the same street address, no two websites can have the same domain. That's why you have to buy and register a domain name — you're claiming that address as yours.

### How to Buy a Domain

Getting a domain is surprisingly simple:

1. **Pick a registrar** — these are companies that sell domains. Popular ones include Namecheap, Google Domains, and Cloudflare Registrar.
2. **Search for your domain** — type in the name you want and see if it's available.
3. **Choose an extension** — `.com` is the most popular, but there's also `.org`, `.net`, `.io`, and hundreds more.
4. **Purchase it** — domains cost about $10-15 per year. You're renting the name, not buying it permanently.
5. **Renew it** — don't forget to renew! If your domain expires, someone else can grab it.

You don't need to buy a domain right now. Just know the process for when you're ready.

## Hosting: Where Your Files Live

**Hosting** is the computer where your website's files are stored. Think of it like **renting an apartment for your website**.

Your website's files (HTML, CSS, JavaScript) need to live on a computer that's always turned on and always connected to the internet. That computer is called a **server**.

Most people don't run their own servers — they pay a hosting company to do it. It's like renting an apartment instead of building your own house.

### Types of Hosting

Not all hosting is the same. Here are the main types, from simplest to most powerful:

- **Shared hosting** — like living in an apartment building. Your website shares a server with hundreds of others. Cheap ($3-10/month) but slower. Good for small sites.
- **VPS (Virtual Private Server)** — like renting a condo. You get your own dedicated space on a server. More power and control ($20-80/month).
- **Managed WordPress hosting** — like a luxury apartment with a concierge. The hosting company handles updates, security, and backups for you. Great for WordPress sites ($15-50/month). Examples: WP Engine, Kinsta.
- **Cloud hosting** — like renting office space that grows as you need it. Pay for what you use. Can handle massive traffic. Examples: AWS, Google Cloud, Azure.

For most small websites and automation projects, shared or managed hosting is more than enough.

## DNS: The Phone Book of the Internet

Here's the tricky part: computers don't actually understand domain names like "google.com". They use **numbers** called IP addresses, like `142.250.80.46`.

**DNS** (Domain Name System) is like a **phone book** that translates names into numbers. When you type "google.com", DNS looks up the number for that name so your browser knows which computer to talk to.

## SSL/HTTPS: The Padlock

You've probably noticed a **padlock icon** in your browser's address bar. That means the website uses **HTTPS** (the "S" stands for Secure).

HTTPS encrypts the data between your browser and the server. Think of it like sending a letter in a **sealed envelope** instead of a postcard — nobody can read it in transit.

- **HTTP** — postcard. Anyone handling it could read it.
- **HTTPS** — sealed envelope. Only the sender and receiver can read it.

Almost all websites use HTTPS now. If you see a website without the padlock, be cautious — especially with login forms or payment pages.

SSL certificates (what makes HTTPS work) used to be expensive, but now they're usually **free** through services like Let's Encrypt. Most hosting companies set them up automatically.

## CDN: Copies Around the World

A **CDN** (Content Delivery Network) is like having **copies of your website stored in cities around the world**.

Without a CDN, if your server is in New York and someone visits from Tokyo, the files have to travel across the Pacific Ocean. That's slow.

With a CDN, a copy of your website is stored in Tokyo, London, Sydney, and dozens of other locations. Visitors get the files from the closest copy, which is much faster.

Popular CDNs include Cloudflare, Amazon CloudFront, and Fastly. Many hosting companies include a CDN automatically.

## What Happens When You Visit a Website

Let's put it all together. When you type `google.com` and press Enter:

1. **DNS lookup** — Your browser asks DNS: "What's the number for google.com?" DNS replies: "142.250.80.46"
2. **Connect** — Your browser connects to the computer at that address
3. **SSL handshake** — Your browser and the server agree on encryption (if HTTPS)
4. **Download** — That computer (the server/host) sends back the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files
5. **Display** — Your browser assembles those files into the page you see

All of this happens in a fraction of a second, every single time you visit any website.

## Try It Yourself: Look Up DNS Records

You can see the DNS records for any domain using a free tool:

1. Go to [dnschecker.org](https://dnschecker.org)
2. Type in any domain — try `google.com`
3. Look at the **A records** — these show the IP addresses where the website lives
4. Try a few different domains and compare the results

You can also do this from your terminal:

```bash
nslookup google.com
```

This shows you the IP address that "google.com" points to. It's the DNS lookup happening right before your eyes!

## Why This Matters for Automation

When you automate tasks later, you'll sometimes need to:

- **Connect to APIs** using domain names (like `api.openai.com`)
- **Understand error messages** like "DNS resolution failed" (now you know — it couldn't look up the address!)
- **Know where data lives** — on which server, at which address
- **Troubleshoot connection issues** — is it a DNS problem? A hosting problem? An SSL issue?

These aren't concepts you need to memorize perfectly. Just having a mental picture of how it works will help you troubleshoot when things don't go as planned.

## Tips and Common Mistakes

- **Domain vs hosting are separate things** — you can buy a domain from one company and host your site with another. They're connected through DNS settings.
- **Don't buy extras at checkout** — domain registrars often upsell "privacy protection" or "website builders." You usually don't need these.
- **Always set up HTTPS** — there's no reason not to in 2026. Most hosting companies do it for free.
- **DNS changes take time** — when you change DNS settings, it can take 1-48 hours for the change to spread across the internet. This is called "propagation."

## Practice Scenario

Pick two websites you use regularly:
1. Look up their DNS records using dnschecker.org
2. Do they use a CDN? (Hint: if the IP addresses are from Cloudflare or Fastly, they're using a CDN)
3. Check if they use HTTPS — look for the padlock icon
4. Look at the domain extension — is it `.com`, `.org`, `.io`, or something else?

This builds your intuition for how websites are set up behind the scenes.
