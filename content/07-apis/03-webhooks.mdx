# Webhooks — When Apps Call You

So far, you've learned about APIs where **you** ask for information. But what if the information could come to **you** automatically? That's what webhooks do.

## The Difference: Calling vs. Being Called

Let's go back to our restaurant analogy.

**APIs are like calling the restaurant** to ask if your takeout order is ready. You call, they check, they tell you. If it's not ready, you have to call again later. And again. And again. That's a lot of phone calls.

**Webhooks are like the restaurant calling YOU** when your food is ready. You give them your phone number, and they ring you the moment it's done. No repeated calls needed.

The simple version: **"Don't call us — we'll call you."**

## Webhook vs. Polling: Mailbox vs. Doorbell

Here is another way to think about it. Imagine you are waiting for a package.

**Polling** (the API approach) is like walking to your mailbox every 5 minutes to check if the package arrived. You waste a lot of trips, and most of the time the mailbox is empty.

**Webhooks** (the smart approach) are like having a doorbell. The delivery person rings it the moment they drop off the package. You don't check at all — you just get notified.

Polling wastes time and resources. Webhooks are instant and efficient. That is why almost every automation tool uses webhooks as triggers.

## How Webhooks Work

A webhook is surprisingly simple. It only needs two things:

1. **A URL to send data to** — this is like giving the restaurant your phone number. You set up a web address that's ready to receive information.
2. **An event that triggers it** — this is the "when." When the food is ready. When a payment is received. When someone pushes code.

When the event happens, the service automatically sends data to your URL. No asking required.

## Real-World Webhook Examples

Webhooks are everywhere. Here are some you might encounter, with the step-by-step flow:

### 1. Stripe Payment Received
1. Customer enters credit card on your website
2. Stripe processes the payment
3. Stripe sends a webhook to your server with payment details
4. Your server updates the order status to "paid"
5. Customer sees "Payment confirmed!" on screen

### 2. GitHub Code Push
1. Developer pushes code to GitHub
2. GitHub sends a webhook to your deployment service
3. The service automatically rebuilds your website
4. New version goes live in minutes — no human needed

### 3. Shopify New Order
1. Customer places an order on your Shopify store
2. Shopify sends a webhook to your warehouse system
3. Warehouse system creates a packing slip
4. Warehouse staff sees the new order and starts packing

### 4. Form Submission (Typeform, Gravity Forms)
1. Someone fills out your contact form
2. The form service sends a webhook to your automation tool
3. The automation sends a confirmation email and logs the data
4. You get a Slack notification with the form details

### 5. Calendar Event (Calendly)
1. Someone books a meeting on your Calendly page
2. Calendly sends a webhook to your CRM
3. The CRM creates a new contact record
4. A prep document is automatically generated for the meeting

## Webhook Security: Staying Safe

When you set up a webhook, you are essentially saying "send data to this URL." But how do you know the data is really from Stripe or GitHub, and not from a hacker pretending to be them?

Most services include a **signature** with their webhooks — a special code that proves the message is genuine. It works like a wax seal on a letter. If the seal is broken or missing, you know something is wrong.

You do not need to build this yourself. Automation tools like n8n, Make, and Zapier verify webhook signatures automatically. But it is good to know the concept:

- **Signature verification** — the service signs each webhook with a secret key, and your server checks the signature before trusting the data
- **HTTPS only** — always use HTTPS URLs for webhooks so the data is encrypted in transit
- **IP allowlisting** — some services publish the IP addresses their webhooks come from, so you can block everything else

## Testing Webhooks

How do you test a webhook before connecting it to your real workflow? Use a free tool called **webhook.site**.

1. Go to [webhook.site](https://webhook.site)
2. You will get a unique URL (something like `https://webhook.site/abc-123-def`)
3. Paste that URL into any service as a webhook endpoint
4. Trigger the event (submit a form, make a test payment, etc.)
5. Go back to webhook.site and see the data that was sent

This is a great way to inspect what data a webhook sends before you build your automation around it.

## Why Webhooks Matter for Automation

Here's the big idea: **webhooks are the foundation of automation**.

Every automation starts with a trigger — "when THIS happens, do THAT." Webhooks are the "when THIS happens" part.

- When a form is submitted &rarr; send an email
- When a payment is received &rarr; create an invoice
- When a blog post is published &rarr; share it on social media

Without webhooks, you'd have to constantly check if something happened. With webhooks, you get notified the instant it does.

## APIs vs. Webhooks: A Quick Comparison

| | APIs | Webhooks |
|---|---|---|
| **Who starts it?** | You do | They do |
| **How it works** | You ask for data | Data is sent to you |
| **Analogy** | Calling the restaurant | Restaurant calls you |
| **Best for** | Getting data when you need it | Reacting to events instantly |
| **Efficiency** | You might check many times for nothing | Only fires when something happens |

Most automation workflows use **both**: webhooks to detect when something happens, and APIs to take action in response.

## Try It Yourself

Visit [webhook.site](https://webhook.site) and grab your unique URL. Then:

1. Open a new browser tab
2. Type your webhook.site URL directly in the address bar and hit Enter
3. Go back to the webhook.site tab — you will see a new request appear
4. Click on it to see the details: headers, body, timestamp

You just sent your first webhook! In a real workflow, the data would come from a form or payment system instead of your browser — but the concept is exactly the same.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using HTTP instead of HTTPS.** Always use HTTPS for webhook URLs. HTTP sends data unencrypted, which is a security risk.
- **Forgetting to handle retries.** If your server is temporarily down, most services will retry the webhook a few times. Make sure your workflow can handle receiving the same data twice.
- **Not testing first.** Always test your webhook with a tool like webhook.site before connecting it to your real systems.

## Key Takeaway

Webhooks are the opposite of APIs. Instead of you asking for information, the information comes to you. They're the "trigger" that makes automation possible — when something happens in one app, a webhook notifies another app to take action.
