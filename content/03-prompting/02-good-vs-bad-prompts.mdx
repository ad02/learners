# Good vs Bad Prompts

The quality of what AI gives you depends entirely on **how you ask**. A vague question gets a vague answer. A specific question gets a useful answer.

Let's look at the difference.

## Example 1: Marketing Post

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write something about marketing"

This is like walking into a restaurant and saying "food please." You'll get *something*, but probably not what you wanted.

**Good prompt:**
> "You are a marketing expert. Write a 200-word LinkedIn post about why small businesses should use automation. Use a professional but friendly tone."

See the difference? The good prompt tells AI **who to be**, **what to write**, **how long**, and **what tone**. The result will be something you can actually use.

## Example 2: Email Writing

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write an email"

**Good prompt:**
> "Write a short email to a client named Sarah. Thank her for the meeting yesterday and confirm our next call is Tuesday at 2pm. Keep it warm and professional."

The good version gives AI **context** (who, what happened) and **format** (short, warm, professional).

## Example 3: Summarizing

**Bad prompt:**
> "Summarize this"

**Good prompt:**
> "Summarize this article in 5 bullet points. Focus on the main takeaways a business owner would care about."

The good version tells AI **how** to summarize (bullet points) and **for whom** (a business owner).

## Example 4: Problem Solving

**Bad prompt:**
> "Help me with my project"

**Good prompt:**
> "I'm planning a team offsite for 15 people in Austin, Texas, in June. Our budget is $5,000. Suggest 3 venue options with pros and cons for each."

The good version gives AI **specific details** so it can give you **specific answers**.

## Example 5: Writing an Email to Your Boss

**Bad prompt:**
> "Write an email asking for time off"

**Good prompt:**
> "Write a professional email to my manager, Lisa. I want to request 3 days off from June 15-17 for a family event. My current projects are on track and my teammate Jake can cover urgent items. Keep the tone polite and confident — not overly apologetic."

The bad version gives AI almost nothing to work with. The good version includes the **who** (Lisa), **what** (3 days off), **when** (June 15-17), **context** (projects on track, Jake can cover), and **tone** (polite, confident). The result will be ready to send with minimal editing.

## Example 6: Creating a Social Media Post

**Bad prompt:**
> "Make a social media post"

**Good prompt:**
> "Write an Instagram caption for a photo of our team at a volunteer event at the local food bank. Our company is a small marketing agency called BrightSpark. The tone should be warm and genuine — not corporate. Include 2-3 relevant hashtags. Keep it under 150 words."

Without specifics, AI will write something generic that sounds like every other post online. With details about **the event**, **the company**, **the platform**, and **the tone**, you get something that actually sounds like your brand.

## The Specificity Scale

Rate your prompts on a scale of 1-10:

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1-2 | Extremely vague | "Help me" |
| 3-4 | Has a topic but no details | "Write about marketing" |
| 5-6 | Has a task and some context | "Write a marketing email for my business" |
| 7-8 | Clear task, good context, format specified | "Write a 200-word email promoting our summer sale to existing customers" |
| 9-10 | Role, full context, specific task, format, tone, constraints | "You are a copywriter. Write a 200-word email for our existing customers about our summer sale (30% off all items, June 1-15). Tone: excited but not pushy. Include a clear call-to-action button text." |

**Aim for 7 or higher** on everything you send to AI. The extra 30 seconds you spend writing a better prompt saves you minutes of editing a bad response.

## What Makes a Prompt Good?

Every good prompt has three things:

1. **Specificity** — tell AI exactly what you want, not something general
2. **Context** — give background so AI understands the situation
3. **Format** — describe how you want the answer to look

## Common Beginner Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Being Too Vague

"Write something good" is not a prompt — it's a wish. AI needs specifics to give you something useful. The more details you provide, the less editing you'll do afterward.

### Mistake 2: Being Too Polite (at the Expense of Clarity)

Some people write prompts like: "Hi! If you don't mind, could you maybe possibly help me with writing an email? Only if it's not too much trouble! Thank you so much!"

AI doesn't have feelings. You don't need to be polite — you need to be **clear**. "Write a professional email to a client confirming our meeting on Tuesday at 2pm" works much better.

> **Pro tip:** Being polite is fine and won't hurt anything, but don't let politeness replace clarity. Say what you need directly.

### Mistake 3: Not Giving Enough Context

AI doesn't know anything about you, your company, your audience, or your situation unless you tell it. Prompts like "Write a blog post for my website" force AI to guess everything. Who is your audience? What industry are you in? What tone does your brand use? You know the answers — share them.

### Mistake 4: Expecting Perfection on the First Try

Even with a great prompt, you'll usually need to adjust the result. That's normal and expected. We'll cover this in the "Iterating Prompts" lesson.

### Mistake 5: Giving Too Many Instructions at Once

If you ask AI to do 10 things in one prompt, the quality of each individual thing drops. Break complex tasks into smaller prompts. First write the outline, then expand each section, then polish the tone.

## Try It Yourself

Take each of these bad prompts and rewrite them to be specific. Add details about who the AI should be, what you need, how long it should be, and what tone to use.

1. **Bad:** "Write a report"
   **Your rewrite:** ___

2. **Bad:** "Help me with a presentation"
   **Your rewrite:** ___

3. **Bad:** "Create content for my business"
   **Your rewrite:** ___

Here's an example rewrite for #1 to get you started:

> "You are a business analyst. Write a 1-page summary report on our Q1 sales performance. We're a 10-person software company that sells project management tools. Revenue was $150K (up 20% from last quarter). Focus on key wins and one area for improvement. Use a professional tone and include bullet points."

See how much more useful that is than "write a report"?

## Practice Scenario

Open Claude (or any AI tool) and try these two prompts back to back:

1. First, send: "Tell me about productivity"
2. Then, send: "You are a productivity coach for small business owners. Give me 5 specific productivity tips that can be implemented today without any apps or tools. For each tip, explain why it works and give a real example. Keep each tip under 50 words."

Compare the responses. The second one will be dramatically more useful — and that's the power of a good prompt.
