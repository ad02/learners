# The Prompt Formula

Now that you've seen good and bad prompts, let's learn a simple formula you can use every time. Think of it as a **recipe** — follow the steps, get a great result.

## The 4-Part Formula

Every great prompt has four parts:

1. **Role** — Who should AI pretend to be?
2. **Context** — What's the situation?
3. **Task** — What do you need done?
4. **Format** — How should the answer look?

Let's break each one down.

## Part 1: Role

Start by telling AI **who to be**. This sets the tone and expertise level.

> "You are a **friendly HR manager**..."
> "You are an **experienced copywriter**..."
> "You are a **patient teacher** explaining to a beginner..."

Why does this matter? If you ask a "marketing expert" to write a post, the result sounds different than if you ask a "casual blogger." The role shapes everything.

## Part 2: Context

Give AI the **background** it needs. What's the situation? Who is this for? Why does it matter?

> "...I'm preparing for a **client presentation next Monday**..."
> "...Our company sells **handmade candles online**..."
> "...This is for **new employees** who just started..."

Without context, AI has to guess. And guesses are usually wrong.

## Part 3: Task

Tell AI **exactly what to do**. Be specific about the action.

> "...Write a welcome email..."
> "...Create a comparison table..."
> "...Explain the difference between X and Y..."

Vague tasks like "help me with this" force AI to guess what you actually need. Clear tasks get clear results.

## Part 4: Format

Describe **how the answer should look**. This saves you from having to reformat everything yourself.

> "...in **5 bullet points**"
> "...as a **table with 3 columns**"
> "...in **under 200 words**, using **simple language**"

## Putting It All Together

Here's the formula in action:

> **Role:** "You are a customer service trainer."
> **Context:** "I run a small online store that sells pet supplies. We get about 50 support emails per day."
> **Task:** "Write 5 email templates for the most common customer complaints."
> **Format:** "Each template should be under 100 words, friendly but professional, and include a placeholder for the customer's name."

That single prompt will give you something you can actually use at work — right away.

## Three More Complete Examples

### Example 1: Meeting Preparation

> **Role:** "You are an executive assistant."
> **Context:** "I'm meeting with a potential client, Greenfield Construction, next Thursday. They're a mid-size construction company considering switching to our project management software. Their main pain point is tracking subcontractor schedules."
> **Task:** "Create a meeting agenda and 5 discovery questions I should ask to understand their needs."
> **Format:** "Agenda as a numbered list with time estimates. Questions as bullet points with a note on why each question is important."

### Example 2: Social Media Content

> **Role:** "You are a social media manager for a small business."
> **Context:** "Our company, FreshBrew Coffee, is a local coffee shop in Portland. Our audience is 25-40 year olds who care about sustainability. We just started composting all our coffee grounds."
> **Task:** "Write 3 Instagram post captions announcing our new composting program."
> **Format:** "Each caption should be under 100 words, include 3-5 hashtags, and end with a question to encourage engagement. Give me one casual version, one educational version, and one funny version."

### Example 3: Data Analysis

> **Role:** "You are a business analyst."
> **Context:** "I have monthly sales data for our 3 product lines: Widgets ($45K), Gadgets ($32K), and Tools ($18K). Last month the numbers were $42K, $35K, and $20K respectively."
> **Task:** "Analyze the month-over-month changes and identify which product line needs attention."
> **Format:** "Present as a brief summary (3-4 sentences) followed by a table showing the numbers, changes, and percentage changes. End with one actionable recommendation."

## The Pizza Analogy

Think of it like ordering food. You don't walk into a restaurant and say **"food please."** You say:

- **Role:** "I'd like to order from the pizza menu" (what kind of food)
- **Context:** "It's for a kids' birthday party" (the situation)
- **Task:** "One large pepperoni pizza" (what you want)
- **Format:** "Extra cheese, no olives, cut into small squares" (how you want it)

The more detail you give, the closer the result matches what you actually need.

## Cheat Sheet: The Prompt Formula

Save this reference and use it every time you write a prompt:

```
ROLE:    "You are a [job title/expert type]..."
CONTEXT: "The situation is [background details]..."
TASK:    "[Specific action verb] [what you need]..."
FORMAT:  "Present it as [structure, length, tone]..."
```

**Quick-start template you can copy and fill in:**

> You are a [role]. I need help with [context/situation]. Please [specific task]. Format the response as [format details]. The tone should be [tone]. Keep it [length constraint].

## Advanced Tips

Once you're comfortable with the basic formula, try these power-ups:

### Chain of Thought

Ask AI to **think through the problem step by step** before giving its answer. This leads to better reasoning.

> "Think through this step by step before giving your final answer: What's the best pricing strategy for our new product?"

### Few-Shot Examples

Give AI **examples of what you want** so it can match the pattern:

> "Write product descriptions in this style:
> Example: 'The CozyMug keeps your coffee warm for 4 hours. Made from recycled ceramic. Dishwasher safe. $24.99'
> Now write descriptions for these 3 products: [list products]"

This works incredibly well because AI is great at matching patterns. The more examples you give, the closer the output matches your style.

### Constraints

Tell AI what **not** to do:

> "Don't use jargon. Don't exceed 200 words. Don't include any technical terms. Don't use bullet points — write in paragraph form."

Sometimes telling AI what to avoid is as important as telling it what to include.

## Common Mistakes with the Formula

> **Watch out for:** Skipping the Role. Without a role, AI defaults to "generic helpful assistant." A specific role dramatically improves the quality.

> **Watch out for:** Vague format instructions. "Make it look nice" doesn't mean anything to AI. "Present as a numbered list with bold headers" does.

> **Pro tip:** You don't need all four parts for every prompt. Simple questions like "What's the capital of France?" don't need a formula. Use the formula for tasks where quality and specificity matter.

## Practice Time

Try building a prompt using all four parts of the formula. Here are three scenarios — pick one and write a complete prompt:

1. You need to write a job posting for a marketing coordinator position at your company
2. You need to create a training document explaining how to use your company's expense reporting system
3. You need to draft a proposal for your boss explaining why the team should adopt a new project management tool

Write your prompt with all four parts: Role, Context, Task, and Format. Then send it to Claude or any AI tool and see what you get. Was the result usable? If not, what part of the formula could you improve?
